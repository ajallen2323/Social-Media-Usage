Introduction

In particular, I am interested in using it to analyze the scientific question of How does gender and age affect time spent on devices through social media?

Results

Due to our response variable being time spent which is continuous, we will fit a simple linear regression model for time spent on a device on age group and gender. The Simple Linear Regression model assumes: \[Y_i \stackrel{ind}{\sim} N(\beta_0 + \beta_1X_i,\sigma^2)\] for \(i = 1\),, n or , equivalently, \[Y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1X_i + \epsilon_i\] \[\epsilon_i\stackrel{ind}{\sim} N(0,\sigma^2)\] where, for observations \(i\),

  • \(Y_i\) is the value of the response variable
  • \(X_i\) is the value of the explanatory variable
  • \(\epsilon_i\) is the error

The model parameters are

  • the intercept \(\beta_0\)
  • the slop \(\beta_1\)
  • the error variance \(\sigma^2\)

Where the response variable (\(Y_i\)) is time spent on a device, the first explanatory(\(X_i1\)) is age group (teen, young adult, adult), the second explanatory(\(X_i2\)) is gender (female, male, non-binary) and the error term (\(\epsilon_i\)) assumed to follow a normal distribution with mean 0 and variance \(\sigma^2\).

Now, we will evaluate the interaction between age group and gender to see if it is significant enough to keep in the model. Below is a plot of the estimated mean time spent on a device with 95% confidence intervals with interaction included. When looking at the graph, we see crossed-line segments, which we would expect a test for interaction to be significant. Though, looking at the ANOVA for our model, p \(\approx\)(0.8342097). The p-value does not show evidence of an interaction. This could be because the data has a lot of noise or variability. Or, since the sample for one level is significantly smaller, it may not provide enough statistical power to detect an interaction effect.

Since the interaction between gender and age group is not significant we will not keep the interaction in the model.

A plot of the estimated mean time spent on a device and 95% confidence intervals are provided below. Similarly, as we saw in the original data, the estimated mean of time spent on a device for a teen among all genders has more variability than the estimated mean for young adults and adults among all genders. Specifically for teens, females have a higher estimated mean time spent on a device than males and non-binary. Another notable observation is that the estimated mean time spent on a device decreases as people age.

Discussion

The social media usage data was generated from Pythons NumPy library. Our goal was to see how gender and age affect time spent on devices through social media. Precisely, we looked at a simple linear regression model of the effect of gender(male, female, non-binary) and age(teen, young adult, adult) on the estimated mean time spent (hours). We found that the estimated means for young adults and adults were very similar across the genders, and for teens, it was higher with more variability between the genders. The diagnostic plot shows a clear pattern in the data points, potentially indicating a violation of the independence assumption. Since we are working with two categorical explanatory variables, grouping can be in effect, which may show patterns or trends. The qq-plot of the residuals might indicate some violation of the normality assumption due to the tail ends of the data veering off the line. Though, to some, this might be a reasonable qq-plot. When looking at the variability between the combinations, we noticed early in the original plot that teens for all genders are more spread out than young adults and adults. Now, looking at the residual vs fitted plot, there does not seem to be any source of evidence violating the constant variance assumption.

Just because the NumPy library generated the data does not inherently imply that it is randomized. This ultimately depends on how the author picked to generate the data and what pattern or distribution was used. If we collected our data from a random sample of people at a random age and gender, we could claim that age and gender caused the difference in time spent on a device. Additionally, since this is not randomly assigned, we must be careful in extending these results to a larger population, especially when some groups in our data need a larger sample size for that group.
